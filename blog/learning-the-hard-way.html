Learning things the hard way and why clarity matters

I learned most things by necessity, not by plan.

When I first became interested in business, I did not have access to mentors, courses, or formal training. Living in Zambia, that is not unusual. If you want to learn something, you often have to piece it together yourself. So I taught myself. I read textbooks meant for classrooms I was not in. I watched long videos, paused them, replayed parts I did not understand, and tried to apply the ideas on my own.

At the time, it felt inefficient. Looking back, it was the only option I had.

I learned design the same way. Not because I wanted to become a designer, but because I could not afford one. I needed logos, layouts, and visuals, and hiring someone simply was not realistic. So I opened design software and started experimenting. The first results were bad. Spacing was off. Colors clashed. Nothing felt consistent.

Slowly, patterns started to emerge. What worked. What didn‚Äôt. The work improved, but only after a lot of trial and error.

Building websites followed a similar path. Drag-and-drop builders were expensive. WordPress felt heavy and overly technical for what I needed at the time. I wanted control without the overhead, so I started figuring things out on my own. I broke things. I rebuilt them. I copied patterns, adjusted them, and learned just enough to move forward.

If you are building things in Zambia, you learn quickly that you cannot always throw money at problems. You improvise, adapt, and keep moving. That mindset shaped how I learned.

More recently, I started using AI to help me code. Not because I was looking for shortcuts, but because it made building possible without full development teams or complex tools. Even then, it was not smooth. Things worked halfway. Then stopped. Then worked again. Progress came with frustration attached.

None of this was clean or linear.

For a long time, I thought the struggle meant I was doing something wrong. That I was behind. That if I were smarter or more prepared, things would feel easier. I was busy most of the time, but I was not always sure I was moving in the right direction.

Eventually, I started noticing a pattern.

Whenever things felt stuck, it was rarely because I lacked effort. It was usually because I lacked clarity. There were too many ideas competing at once. Too many things felt urgent. Without a clear priority, every decision felt heavier than it needed to be.

Once I saw that, it explained a lot.

Clarity did not come from learning more tools. It came from slowing down. From asking simpler questions before starting again. What is this for. What problem am I actually solving. What can wait.

When those questions were clear, progress felt lighter. Not faster, but steadier. I started fewer things and finished more of them.

Today, clarity shapes how I work more than ambition or speed. I still learn things the hard way at times. That part has not disappeared. But I am quicker to recognize when confusion is the real problem. Before adding complexity, I try to remove noise. Before building more, I try to understand better.

Most of what I know came from not having options. Building in Zambia forces that kind of learning. You either figure things out or you stay stuck. I paid for that education with time and mistakes. What it gave me was a way of thinking that values clarity, especially when resources are limited.
